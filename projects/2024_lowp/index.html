Mechanisms of Gas Generation during Thermal Runaway of Lithium-ion Batteries under Complex Abusive Conditions

Calculation of kinetics parameters of gas generation reactions with different ambient pressures {1.0 atm, 0.6 atm, 0.2 atm} and different charging rates {2 C, 3 C, 4 C}.

Overview Link to heading

With the increased demand for lithium-ion batteries (LIBs) in aviation and high-altitude applications, understanding battery safety under low-pressure conditions is increasingly crucial. Unlike thermal abuse, electrical abuse involves electrochemical impacts, such as lithium plating and solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) film consumption and regeneration, which affect LIB failure mechanisms. However, the complex effects of electrical abuse combined with low ambient pressures on thermal runaway (TR) behaviors remain unaddressed.

To fill this gap, this study investigates the impacts of ambient pressure {0.2 atm, 0.6 atm, 1.0 atm} and charging rate {2 C, 3 C, 4 C} on the TR behaviors of commercial 18650-type cells induced by electrical abuse. This investigation employs an accelerating rate calorimeter (ARC) and an airtight jar. The results reveal that low ambient pressure leads to earlier safety valve opening and a shortened delay time for TR, limiting electrolyte ejection and increasing both maximum temperature and gas generation. Additionally, more flammable gases are generated under low-pressure conditions. Elevated charging rates further intensify the TR process, increasing flammable gas generation and potential hazards.

Numerical models are essential for understanding and predicting TR behaviors. Despite extensive research on thermal kinetic parameters for side reactions during TR, there is limited understanding of gas generation dynamics, especially under low-pressure conditions. The Arrhenius law is used to evaluate gas generation dynamics by comparing activation energy and pre-exponential factors. The study proposes rate constants for various operation pressures and charging rates, with notable increases in rate constants at 0.2 atm compared to 1.0 atm, highlighting the higher TR risk under low-pressure conditions.
